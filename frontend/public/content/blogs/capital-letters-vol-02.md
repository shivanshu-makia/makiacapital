In 2020, I made a concentrated bet on Tesla. Not because of Elon Musk's Twitter presence, not because of the stock's momentum, and certainly not because someone told me to. I had a thesis.

The thesis was straightforward: Tesla was transitioning from a niche EV manufacturer to a vertically integrated energy company. The market was pricing it as a car company. I believed the gap between perception and reality would close — violently.

## The Volatility Test

What I didn't anticipate was how violently the journey would test that thesis. Within six months of my entry, the stock dropped 35%. Every morning, I opened my portfolio to a sea of red. The WhatsApp groups I was in — the same ones I'd later write about in Vol. 01 — were full of people panicking, exiting, declaring Tesla 'over.'

But here's what separated a thesis from a tip: I had written down exactly what would make me sell. And none of those conditions had been met.

## Conviction vs. Stubbornness

This is the part most investors get wrong. They confuse conviction with stubbornness. Conviction is holding a position because your thesis remains intact despite price action. Stubbornness is holding a position because you refuse to admit you were wrong.

The difference is documentation. If you've written your thesis and your falsifying conditions, you have a framework for distinguishing between the two. If you haven't, you're guessing.
