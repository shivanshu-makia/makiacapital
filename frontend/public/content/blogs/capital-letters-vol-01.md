My father lost money in 2008 following a tip from a friend at a dinner table. The stock was 'certain to double.' It had a good promoter, someone had seen the quarterly numbers, there was a block deal coming. All the ingredients of conviction — without any of the work.

I was 16. That evening taught me something it took a decade to properly articulate.

## The Tip Economy

The Indian retail investing ecosystem runs on tips. WhatsApp groups, Telegram channels, YouTube thumbnails, Twitter threads — all of it is a tip economy. The format has evolved. The underlying behaviour hasn't.

A tip is information without context, conviction without research, and action without ownership. The person who gives it rarely has skin in the game. The person who acts on it always does.

There's a reason the tip economy is so seductive: it removes the hardest part of investing — thinking. You don't have to read an annual report. You don't have to understand a business model. You don't have to sit with uncertainty. Someone else has done 'the work,' and all you have to do is act.

But that's precisely the problem. The value in investing isn't in the action — it's in the reasoning behind it. When the stock drops 15% in a week, the person who gave you the tip has moved on. You haven't. You're sitting with a loss and no framework to evaluate whether to hold, average down, or exit.

## What a Thesis Actually Looks Like

A thesis is not a view. A view is 'I think the market will go up.' A thesis is 'I believe Company X will deliver 25% earnings CAGR over 3 years because of these specific structural tailwinds, FCF conversion is improving, management has demonstrated capital allocation discipline, and the stock is available at a discount to fair value.'

A thesis has a falsifying condition. You know what would prove you wrong. A tip has none.

When I started Makia, one of the first principles we established was that every investment must be backed by a written thesis. Not a pitch deck summary. Not a one-liner. A full thesis — with clearly articulated risks, a defined exit framework, and a falsifying condition that would make us revisit the position.

## The Dinner Table Test

Here's a simple test I use: if someone gives you a stock idea at a dinner table, ask them three questions:

1. **What does the company actually do?** Not the sector, not the theme — the actual business model and how it makes money.
2. **What's the valuation basis?** Why is this stock worth buying at this price versus six months ago or six months from now?
3. **What would make you sell?** Not a target price — a fundamental condition that would invalidate the thesis.

If they can't answer all three clearly, it's a tip. And tips can't be your thesis.

## What This Means for You

If you cannot write three sentences explaining why you own a stock — the business model, the valuation basis, and the risk — you don't own an investment. You own a bet.

Bets can work. They just can't compound. And compounding is the only game worth playing in markets.

This newsletter exists to build frameworks, not distribute tips. Every edition of Capital Letters will aim to sharpen how you think about markets, businesses, and capital allocation — because in investing, process always wins over prediction.

> If you cannot write three sentences explaining why you own a stock, you don't own an investment. You own a bet.
